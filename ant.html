Lauren Leung
• An Ant Can Hold 50 Times its weight •
• What I Really Want to Say •
• Summer Half A Life Ago •

An ant can carry fifty times its weight, but I stagger under a third and a thought. I drink my coffee too hot so it turns my tongue papery and then I can finally write. Sometimes I go to the beach and stand where the water brushes against the sand. I close my eyes and the sensation of the ground beneath my feet melting away makes me feel weak inside, the feeling of moving without moving. The ocean gives me a fleeting sort of peace, cold and awakening, lasting only as long as the smoothness of my fingertips. Better is the balm of sleep, which I seek endlessly, the salvation in the beaming stars, the far-flung fantasies of dreams chasing away the discontented waking hours. At night, I wish I could pull the mist over the hills just a bit farther, so it would fall around me as I sleep and I wouldn’t have to see the faces of the people hovering, and I wouldn’t have to know if they were coming or leaving. In the mornings, I can barely talk for fear of hearing my own voice. I shield my face from the harsh brightness of the newborn sun and attempt to insert myself into the world once more. Sometimes I do it successfully, and I spend the day present among people, winding tenuous connections to their smiles and hands. Other days, the air is thick so I don’t move, and I hover in a curious state of viewing, watching with distant, clouded eyes as the colors blend together like paint in water and the falling light wavers, turning liquid in the dust. I pay particular attention to how I walk now, how my legs move. I worry that if I forget to concentrate, I may accidentally take a wrong step, and there is no recovery from time, which I always thought was odd because they say that Time is the great healer but they forget that Time was what hurt us all in the first place. If I gasp like the wind is a knife, it’s because the cracks in my heart just grew a bit larger and the hand over my chest is not only holding myself together but feeling for my heartbeat, the one my mother listened to seventeen years ago.

Neha, Palos Verdes. 2019.
